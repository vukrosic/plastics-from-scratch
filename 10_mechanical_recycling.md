# Lesson 10: Mechanical Recycling

Mechanical recycling turns used plastic into new material without breaking the
polymer all the way back into chemical building blocks.

The basic loop is:

```text
collect -> sort -> clean -> shred -> melt -> reform
```

## Why Care About Mechanical Recycling?

Because it is the most intuitive recycling pathway.

People imagine:

```text
old bottle -> new bottle
```

Sometimes that can happen.

But real mechanical recycling depends on clean, consistent material streams.

This lesson explains where the difficulties come from.

## Step 1: Collection

Recycling begins with collection.

Material may come from:

```text
curbside bins
deposit systems
drop-off points
commercial waste streams
industrial scrap
```

Collection determines what enters the system.

If the stream is mixed, contaminated, or poorly sorted at the start, every later
step becomes harder.

## Step 2: Sorting

Sorting separates materials.

Plastic may be sorted by:

```text
polymer type
color
shape
density
size
container type
```

Sorting matters because different plastics often do not mix well.

If the wrong polymer contaminates a batch, the recycled material can become
weaker, cloudy, brittle, or hard to process.

Simple idea:

```text
better sorting -> more predictable recycled plastic
```

## Step 3: Cleaning

Used plastic can contain:

```text
food residue
soap
oil
labels
adhesives
dirt
other materials
```

Cleaning removes as much contamination as practical.

Clean material is easier to process and more valuable.

Dirty material can smell, degrade, discolor, or create defects in new products.

## Step 4: Shredding

After sorting and cleaning, plastic is often shredded into flakes.

Flakes are easier to:

```text
wash
dry
transport
melt evenly
feed into equipment
```

The flake stage is important because it turns irregular objects into a more
uniform material stream.

But the chemistry has not changed much yet.

It is still plastic pieces.

## Step 5: Melting And Reprocessing

Thermoplastic flakes can be melted or softened and processed again.

Common outputs:

```text
pellets
fiber
sheet
new containers
plastic lumber
strapping
parts
```

The exact output depends on material quality.

High-quality sorted streams can become higher-value products.

Mixed or degraded streams may become lower-performance products.

## Step 6: Downcycling

Downcycling means the recycled material becomes a lower-value or
lower-performance product.

Example idea:

```text
clear bottle material -> colored product
food packaging -> non-food application
high-spec material -> lower-spec part
```

Downcycling is not automatically bad.

It can still keep material in use.

But it shows that recycling is not always a perfect closed loop.

## Step 7: Why Quality Drops

Recycled plastic quality can drop because of:

```text
mixed polymers
additives
colors
heat history
moisture
food contamination
chain damage
unknown sources
```

Every recycling pass can add uncertainty.

Some systems manage this very well.

Others produce material that is useful only for less demanding applications.

This is why design for recycling matters.

## Step 8: Closed Loop vs Open Loop

Closed-loop recycling means material returns to a similar product.

Example:

```text
bottle -> bottle
```

Open-loop recycling means material becomes a different product.

Example:

```text
bottle -> fiber
container -> plastic lumber
```

Closed loop is often harder because the quality requirements are stricter.

Food-contact applications can have especially strict requirements.

## Step 9: Mechanical Recycling Model

Use this model:

```text
mechanical recycling works best when:
    material is easy to identify
    material is clean
    material is sorted by polymer
    additives and colors are compatible
    there is demand for the recycled output
```

Recycling is not just a machine problem.

It is a system problem.
