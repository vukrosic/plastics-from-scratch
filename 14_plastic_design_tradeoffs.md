# Lesson 14: Plastic Design Tradeoffs

Plastic design is full of tradeoffs.

There is rarely one perfect material choice.

A good plastic product balances:

```text
performance
cost
manufacturing
safety
weight
durability
recycling
environmental impact
```

## Why Care About Tradeoffs?

Because simple slogans do not design real products.

For example:

```text
make it stronger
make it lighter
make it cheaper
make it recyclable
make it transparent
make it heat resistant
make it biodegradable
```

You may not get all of these at once.

Engineering is choosing the best compromise for the job.

## Step 1: Performance

The material must do the job.

Questions:

```text
does it hold the load?
does it resist impact?
does it survive heat?
does it block moisture or oxygen?
does it keep its shape?
does it feel right to the user?
```

If the material fails, the design fails.

A package that uses less plastic but lets food spoil may not be a better system.

Performance comes first because the product has a job.

## Step 2: Cost

Plastic is widely used partly because it can be low cost.

Cost includes:

```text
raw material
additives
manufacturing time
mold cost
scrap rate
transportation
quality control
end-of-life handling
```

A material that looks better technically may be too expensive for the product.

That does not make cost shallow.

Cost affects whether a design can actually be used.

## Step 3: Weight

Plastics are often lighter than metal, glass, or ceramic alternatives.

Lower weight can help with:

```text
shipping
fuel use
handling
product portability
material efficiency
```

But lightweighting can go too far.

If the product becomes too thin, it may:

```text
crack
leak
deform
fail during shipping
be harder to sort
feel low quality
```

The goal is not always minimum weight.

The goal is enough material in the right place.

## Step 4: Durability

Durability can be good.

Examples:

```text
medical device
car part
pipe
electrical insulation
reusable container
```

Durability can also be a problem.

Examples:

```text
single-use wrapper
disposable fork
thin film litter
uncollected packaging
```

The same property can be good or bad depending on use.

Simple rule:

```text
durability should match product lifetime
```

## Step 5: Recyclability

Design can make recycling easier or harder.

Recycling-friendly design often tries to:

```text
use fewer material types
avoid incompatible layers
choose sortable colors
use removable labels
reduce problematic additives
match local recycling systems
```

But recyclability can conflict with other goals.

For example, a multi-layer package may protect food well but be difficult to
recycle mechanically.

That is a real tradeoff.

## Step 6: Reuse

Reuse can reduce waste when the product is actually reused many times.

But reusable products usually need to be stronger.

That can mean:

```text
more material
more cleaning
more transport weight
different user behavior
```

Reuse is best evaluated as a system.

Question:

```text
how many times will this actually be reused?
```

If the answer is low, the benefit may be smaller than expected.

## Step 7: Material Substitution

Replacing plastic with another material is not automatically better.

Alternatives may be:

```text
heavier
more energy-intensive
more breakable
less protective
harder to transport
harder to recycle locally
```

Sometimes substitution is useful.

Sometimes redesign is better.

Sometimes reduction is better.

The right question is:

```text
what problem are we solving?
```

## Step 8: Design For The Whole Life

A better plastic design considers:

```text
source material
manufacturing
transport
use
reuse
repair
recycling
disposal
leakage risk
```

This is life-cycle thinking.

You do not need a full life-cycle assessment in this course.

But you should think beyond the object in your hand.

Good design asks:

```text
what happens before use?
what happens during use?
what happens after use?
```

## Step 9: Three Quick Questions

Question:

```text
Why is "use less plastic" not always enough as a design rule?
```

Answer:

```text
the product still has to work, protect contents, and fit the whole system
```

Question:

```text
Why can durability be both good and bad?
```

Answer:

```text
it is good for long-use products but problematic for short-use waste
```

Question:

```text
What does life-cycle thinking ask?
```

Answer:

```text
what happens before, during, and after use
```
