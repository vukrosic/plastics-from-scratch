# Lesson 13: Microplastics

Microplastics are small plastic particles.

They can come from larger plastic items breaking apart.

They can also be released already small.

This lesson explains the basic sources and why size matters.

## Why Care About Microplastics?

Because plastic waste is not only a visible bottle or bag problem.

Plastic can fragment into smaller pieces that are harder to collect, track, and
remove.

Once particles are small, they can move through:

```text
water
soil
air
food systems
organisms
```

Microplastics are a systems problem, not just a litter problem.

## Step 1: What Counts As Microplastic?

A common definition is plastic particles smaller than:

```text
5 mm
```

That is about the size of a small seed or smaller.

Some particles are much smaller than that.

Very tiny particles may be called nanoplastics, though definitions and
measurement methods can vary.

The main idea:

```text
large plastic object -> smaller plastic fragments
```

## Step 2: Primary Microplastics

Primary microplastics are intentionally made small or released as small plastic
particles.

Examples can include:

```text
industrial pellets
some abrasive particles
small manufactured plastic particles
```

Plastic pellets are sometimes called nurdles.

They are raw material used in manufacturing.

If pellets spill during production or transport, they can enter the environment
before becoming a product.

## Step 3: Secondary Microplastics

Secondary microplastics form when larger plastic items break down.

Examples:

```text
bottle fragments
bag fragments
fishing gear fragments
packaging fragments
paint particles
synthetic textile fibers
tire wear particles
```

Sunlight, heat, oxygen, mechanical wear, and waves can weaken plastic.

The object can crack and fragment.

The plastic may not vanish.

It may become many smaller pieces.

## Step 4: Fibers From Textiles

Many clothes contain synthetic fibers.

Examples:

```text
polyester
nylon
acrylic
spandex blends
```

During washing and wearing, tiny fibers can shed.

Some may enter wastewater.

Wastewater treatment can capture many particles, but not everything is removed
in every system.

This is why textiles are part of the microplastics conversation.

Plastic is not only packaging.

## Step 5: Tire Wear

Tires are not simple plastic packaging, but they are polymer-based materials.

As vehicles drive, tire material wears down.

Tiny particles can be released to roads and runoff.

This is a major source often missed by beginners.

Microplastic pollution is not only caused by visible plastic trash.

It can also come from everyday wear.

## Step 6: Why Size Matters

Small particles behave differently from large objects.

They are:

```text
harder to see
harder to collect
easier to transport
easier for organisms to encounter
harder to measure consistently
```

A bottle on a beach can be picked up.

Millions of tiny fragments in sediment or water are much harder.

That is why prevention matters.

## Step 7: Fragmentation Is Not The Same As Disappearance

When plastic breaks into smaller pieces, it may look like it is going away.

But fragmentation is not the same as full chemical breakdown.

Simple distinction:

```text
fragmentation:
    big pieces become small pieces

mineralization:
    material breaks down into simple substances like CO2, water, and biomass
```

Many plastics fragment long before they fully break down.

This is one reason durable plastics create environmental problems.

## Step 8: First Systems Model

Use this model:

```text
plastic source
    -> release
    -> fragmentation or shedding
    -> transport
    -> exposure
    -> possible impact
```

A solution can act at many points:

```text
better product design
less unnecessary use
improved collection
spill prevention
wastewater controls
stormwater controls
cleanup
material substitution where appropriate
```

No single step solves every source.

## Step 9: Three Quick Questions

Question:

```text
What are secondary microplastics?
```

Answer:

```text
small plastic particles formed when larger plastic items break down
```

Question:

```text
Why are small plastic particles harder to manage than bottles or bags?
```

Answer:

```text
they are harder to see, collect, measure, and remove
```

Question:

```text
Is fragmentation the same as complete breakdown?
```

Answer:

```text
no
```
